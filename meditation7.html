How Humility Solves the Problem of Tolerance

Thoughts from John Dickson's "Humilitas"

Desmond Jones
"Tolerance in this sense is illogical and potentially harmful. It is like an inexperienced counselor telling a troubled married couple to ignore their differences and focus on their similarities."

Tolerance. It is the word plastered on a poster on every American college campus. It is the word that is constantly dripping from compromised postmodernists. It is ultimately a word of defeat. 

    As John Dickson points out in his book “Humilitas”, tolerance has come to signify intellectual cowardice. Instead of trying one’s hardest to find the truth, one can merely throw one’s hands in the air and say “It is all equally valid”. But, as Dickson says, “a scientific view of medicine. . . is better than magical approaches to health” (emphasis not mine). There are some things that are simply objectively true: no amount of tolerance can change truth. 

    Tolerance may come from decent hearts. Dickson quotes the United Nations’ statement on tolerance: “It is a fact that in most societies today, many different religions, cultures, and lifestyles coexist. It is essential to recall that the basic human values that unite us are stronger than the forces that pull us apart.” The call for unity in tolerance is attractive: but the call for compromise in its practice reveals tolerance’s fatal flaw. 

    Consider, as Dickson does, how a Christian, under this philosophy, “tolerates” Islam: via epistemic humility (the idea that one cannot be sure of anything one knows), the Christian must simply concede that Islam may be correct the Jesus is merely a prophet, in spite of the fact that Christianity’s foundational principle is that Jesus is the Son of God. One sees how toleration must become acceptance. While this transaction buys us unity, it also costs us backbones.

    How can someone have a spine if they think anyone could be right about anything? Why fight for the oppressed when it is possible that the oppressors are justified? Why defend the rights of women in the Middle East if strict sharia law may be just as valid as Western liberal norms? 

    The solution, according to Dickson, is true humility, which he defines as “a willingness to hold power in service of others”. True humility must be exercised by the “muscle of compassion”, which is separate from the “muscle of moral conviction”. As G.K. Chesterton says, “A man was meant to be doubtful about himself but undoubting about the truth.”

    What does this look like? Disagreeing with your enemy while still earnestly praying for their well-being. Humility in the realm of intellectual conflict is not compromise of principle, but rather compromise of ego.
