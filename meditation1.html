"You needn't be afraid of life!"

Dostoevsky's timeless call to bold love

Desmond Jones
“And whether you are absorbed in the most important pursuits, reaching out for the highest honors, or struck down by the cruelest griefs, always remember how good it felt when we were all here together, united by a good and decent feeling, which made us, while we all loved this boy, better people, probably, than we would otherwise have been.”

In my article on Jack London’s “Sea Wolf” I discussed the implicit call to action found in London’s work: “to sacrifice oneself for another”. Given that this is a fundamentally Christian idea, it is hardly surprising that one of the foremost Christian writers of the 19th century, Fyodor Dostoevsky, should also consider it the primary avenue of meaning in his grand magnum opus “The Brothers Karamazov”. 

The story is ostensibly a legal drama concerning patricide: but any time spent in its examination reveals the novel to be much more. Conversations between Ivan and Alyosha reveal the wider cultural divide in Russia regarding Christian traditionalism and atheist progressivism. The laments of the sensual Dmitri Karamazov become the laments of the spiritually starved and depraved Russian people. “The Brothers Karamazov” is in essence a Platonic dialogue in dramatized and novelized form, wherein every conceivable worldview is paraded out and then attacked.

What is the worldview that survives? The altruistic (and distinctly Christian) mysticism of Alyosha Karamazov. The novel ends with the death of a young child, Ilyusha. The story of Ilyusha, while not central to the plot of the legal drama, is perhaps the most valuable literary depiction of the power of redemptive love. 

Ilyusha, a frail child, is bullied by his schoolmates for defending the honor of his drunkard father. But as Ilyusha’s terminal illness becomes manifest, Alyosha manages to rally the schoolchildren to aid their fellow in his time of need. We begin to see how redemptive love brings the schoolchildren into an intimate bond with Ilyusha and his family. When Ilyusha’s death comes to pass, the children weep as passionately as the family. 

After the funeral, and the boys are in mourning, Alyosha Karamazov gives a speech that is a summation of the intellectual conflict throughout the novel. 

The essence of his speech has been quoted above, and I will repeat some of it now: “always remember how good it felt when we were all here together, united by a good and decent feeling.” Alyosha emphasizes how a memory of innocent goodness has the power to redeem someone from falling to temptation. He also emphasizes the need to carry on in the mission of divine love.

But what strikes the reader most is Alyosha’s exhortation: “You needn’t be afraid of life! Life is so good when you do something that is good and just.” It puts one in mind of the apostle John’s reminder that “perfect love casteth out fear”, serving as another example of the myriad of ways that the apostle influenced Dostoevsky. 

It is here that Dostoevsky makes the same call to action that London does in “Sea Wolf”: only here, it is more evident. We must always remember the times we were good, and just, to remind us of what we can be. We must always seek to observe divine love and consciously emulate it, thereby feeling the compulsion of doing good. In all this, we will see the fear of life be cast aside as we seek to serve something higher.
