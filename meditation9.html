Platonic Love in "Phaedrus"

Plato's take on "heaven's a place on earth"

Desmond Jones
It seemed to Plato that the eternal soul could either leave this life by rising to the celestial heights or sinking to subterranean depths. This idea is hardly original to Plato, but it is what is destined for those in the heavens that can captivate the modern reader.

 Platonic philosophy regarding love is far-reaching and deeply sublime, making it difficult for a novice (like yours truly) to effectively comprehend such a beautiful idea. But Plato gives a potential seed for his readers in two of his dialogues: “Symposium” and “Phaedrus”. In the latter dialogue, only the first half revolves around love, but the ideas expounded in that text alone is sufficient for reflective consideration.

  Plato gives two perspectives on love in “Phaedrus”. The first is given twice, first in a recorded speech by Lysias recited by Phaedrus, the other a speech from Plato’s Socrates. In these two pieces of philosophical rhetoric, the dangers of love are expressed. These dangers are what one might expect: love leads to jealousy, excessive worldliness, vice, and a narrowness of perspective. But these dangers, at least in the eyes of Plato’s Socrates, pale in comparison to love’s inherent divine qualities.

  The Greeks were known for deifying human passions, including love. Plato, through the lips of Socrates, reasoned that in light of the holiness of Eros, the god of love, the human emotion must have a greater meaning. While this reasoning feels insufficient to any modern reader, one must remember the power of psychological archetypes as they are manifest in ancient literature. The fact that the Greeks felt necessary to deify love suggests something significant about the emotion beyond its irrational inconvenience.

  Plato began by considering the soul, the natural habitat of love. He effectively postulates inherent divinity of every soul, for they are all “self-moving” (“ἑαυτοῦ κινουμένου”). Because self-movement implies no beginning, and something which is generated from itself requires nothing else to be sustained, the soul must therefore be immortal and eternal, in the traditional theological senses of those words. While these conclusions are disagreeable to many, the place where they end is interesting, for Plato begins to ask what the fate of these eternal souls are.

  It seemed to Plato that the eternal soul could either leave this life by rising to the celestial heights or sinking to subterranean depths. This idea is hardly original to Plato, but it is what is destined for those in the heavens that can captivate the modern reader. Plato postulated that one could rise to the level of an immaterial realm of being, where perfect justice, wisdom, and beauty can be observed. The Platonist would say this is a fate of beholding the Platonic forms: the Christian may say this is the fate of beholding God. The intersection between these two conceptions of the afterlife is fascinating.

  Regardless, in this life, the soul is tethered to the body, making the perfection of the immaterial realm unreachable. But there lies a consolation to the lover, for the object of the lover’s affections make the heavenly attributes manifest in an earthbound being. It is often said that the lover worships the object of desire, but the worship only occurs in its relation to the absolute of perfection. Love of the beloved, in a sense, prepares us for heaven, according to Plato.
