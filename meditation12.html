How the Myth of Icarus Is an Illustration of Modern Man

Can we reach the heights of wonder in an uncaring universe?

Desmond Jones

 The ancient Greek myth of Icarus has long been seen as a cautionary tale concerning pride and hubris. But the story feels more poignant in this modern age of Ubermensch. Tony Curtis’ “Icarus” reveals how the archetype of Icarus is applicable. In describing an Indian aerial stowaway falling from the sky, Curtis pins down another aspect of the myth: hope. Hope is the element in the story that is what prompts the escape by Daedelus and Icarus. But the story shows the fatal element of hope.

 The myth describes man’s hope to reach unattainable heights and being impotent to finding the consummation of that desire. Landscape With The Fall of Icarus, by WIlliam Carlos Williams, features the line “a splash quite unnoticed”. Therein lies the fatal consequence of hope. In bringing Icarus to the heights, Icarus crashes to the depths, and the universe has no care for his demise. Modern man attempts to reach the heights in a vain hope, but often fails, and are left to face a harsh reality of existence.
