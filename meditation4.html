The First Autobiography: Augustine's Confessions

Thoughts on the work and the author

Desmond Jones
"The Church demanded that certain things should be believed even though they could not be proved, for if they could be proved, not all men could understand the proof, and some could not be proved at all. I thought that the Church was entirely honest in this and far less pretentious than the Manichees, who laughed at people who took things on faith, made rash promises of scientific knowledge, and then put forward a whole system of preposterous inventions which they expected their followers to believe on trust because they could not be proved" (Augustine 116)

  Verbose. Tangential. Unfocused. These are a few of the words I could use to describe St. Augustine’s “Confessions”.

  Allegedly the first autobiography, one would expect the “Confessions” to be a linear story describing Augustine’s intellectual and spiritual journey from Manichaeism to Christianity. Where the “Confessions” matches this expectation, it is a marvelous experience. Unfortunately, he consistently covers immaterial topics that distract from a moving testimony. That being said, this literary quality in Augustine’s work, while certainly being artless, shows an admirable quality in Augustine himself, which deserves to be emulated: endless awareness regarding the self, the world, and God.

  Augustine’s yearning for understanding is something that mustn’t be quashed, but nurtured and developed, for it is understanding that brings fulfillment and progress. Indeed, Augustine’s scrutinous writings reflect the purpose of this publication: to focus on the ellipses society inevitably create.

  Inquisitive. Patient. Aware. These are a few of the words I could use to describe St. Augustine, and I hope they will eventually describe us all.
